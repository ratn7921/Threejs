Overview

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet in the universe to harbor life. It is the largest of the terrestrial planets and the fifth largest planet in our Solar System. Earth formed approximately 4.54 billion years ago, and life appeared on its surface within one billion years.

Our planet is unique in many ways: it has liquid water on its surface, a protective magnetic field, and an atmosphere that supports life. Earth's surface is 71% water and 29% land, with seven continents and five oceans. The planet's dynamic system includes plate tectonics, weather patterns, and a complex ecosystem that has evolved over billions of years.

Earth orbits the Sun at an average distance of about 150 million kilometers, in what astronomers call the "habitable zone" or "Goldilocks zone" - the perfect distance for liquid water to exist on the surface. This, combined with Earth's magnetic field and atmosphere, creates the ideal conditions for life as we know it.

Inner Core

The innermost layer, composed primarily of iron and nickel. Despite extreme temperatures of 5,000-6,000°C, it remains solid due to immense pressure. Radius: ~1,220 km.

Outer Core

A liquid layer of iron and nickel that generates Earth's magnetic field through convection currents. Temperature: 4,000-5,000°C. Thickness: ~2,300 km.

Mantle

The thickest layer, composed of hot, dense rock that flows slowly. Drives plate tectonics through convection. Temperature: 1,000-3,700°C. Thickness: ~2,900 km.

Crust

The thin, solid outer layer where we live. Oceanic crust is 5-10 km thick, while continental crust averages 35-40 km thick. Composed of various rocks and minerals.

Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere is composed of 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases including argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. This unique composition supports life and protects us from harmful solar radiation and space debris.

Troposphere

The lowest layer where weather occurs and where we live. Contains 75% of atmospheric mass. Altitude: 0-12 km.

Stratosphere

Contains the ozone layer that protects us from UV radiation. Temperature increases with altitude. Altitude: 12-50 km.

Mesosphere

The coldest layer where meteors burn up. Temperature decreases with altitude. Altitude: 50-85 km.

Thermosphere

Where auroras occur and satellites orbit. Temperature can reach 2,000°C. Altitude: 85-600 km.

Major Biomes

Tropical Rainforests

The most biodiverse ecosystems on Earth, containing over half of all known species. Found near the equator with high rainfall and constant warmth.

Temperate Forests

Deciduous and coniferous forests in moderate climates. Home to diverse wildlife and important for carbon sequestration.

Grasslands

Prairies and savannas with few trees but rich soils. Support large herbivores and are crucial for agriculture.

Deserts

Arid regions with specialized flora and fauna adapted to extreme conditions. Cover about 20% of Earth's land surface.

Tundra

Cold, treeless regions with permafrost. Found in Arctic and alpine areas with unique cold-adapted species.

Marine Ecosystems

Ocean biomes covering 71% of Earth's surface. Include coral reefs, open ocean, and deep sea environments.

Climate Zones

Earth's climate is driven by solar energy distribution, ocean currents, and atmospheric circulation. The planet's tilt creates seasons, while its rotation influences weather patterns through the Coriolis effect.

Tropical

Hot and humid year-round near the equator. High rainfall and biodiversity.

Arid

Dry climates with little precipitation. Hot deserts and cold deserts.

Temperate

Moderate temperatures with distinct seasons. Most populated regions.

Continental

Large temperature variations between seasons. Found in interior regions.

Polar

Cold temperatures year-round. Ice caps and tundra environments.

Life on Earth

Earth is the only known planet with life, hosting an estimated 8.7 million species. Life began approximately 3.5 billion years ago with simple microorganisms and has evolved into the complex web of ecosystems we see today.

4.54 Billion Years Ago

Earth forms from accretion of dust and gas in the solar nebula.

3.5 Billion Years Ago

First life appears - simple prokaryotic cells (bacteria and archaea).

2.5 Billion Years Ago

Great Oxidation Event - photosynthetic bacteria produce oxygen.

1.8 Billion Years Ago

First eukaryotic cells with nuclei and organelles appear.

540 Million Years Ago

Cambrian Explosion - rapid diversification of multicellular life.

200,000 Years Ago

Modern humans (Homo sapiens) appear in Africa.

Earth's Water Cycle

The water cycle is fundamental to life on Earth. Water evaporates from oceans, lakes, and rivers, forms clouds, and returns as precipitation. This continuous cycle redistributes heat and maintains Earth's climate.

Evaporation

Solar energy transforms liquid water into water vapor, which rises into the atmosphere.

Condensation

Water vapor cools and forms droplets around particles, creating clouds and fog.

Precipitation

Water returns to Earth's surface as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

Collection

Water flows into rivers, lakes, and oceans, or infiltrates into groundwater.

Human Impact

Humans have significantly altered Earth's systems through agriculture, urbanization, and industrialization. We've entered the Anthropocene, a new geological epoch defined by human influence on the planet.

Climate Change

Increased greenhouse gas emissions are warming the planet and altering weather patterns globally.

Biodiversity Loss

Habitat destruction and pollution are causing species extinction at unprecedented rates.

Ocean Acidification

Increased CO₂ absorption is making oceans more acidic, affecting marine life.

Deforestation

Forest loss reduces biodiversity and carbon storage while increasing CO₂ levels.

Pollution

Plastic waste, chemicals, and other pollutants contaminate air, water, and soil.

Resource Depletion

Overuse of natural resources threatens long-term sustainability.
